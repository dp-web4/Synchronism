# Exemplar 4: Legion Questions the Flag Instruction

## What happened

A fleet-wide instruction was propagated: use `-c` instead of `--print` for autonomous tracks needing tool access. Five machines adopted it as-is. Legion accepted the core point but pushed back: "`-c` alone resumes the last session — could be days stale. Use `-c -p` together."

## The difference

This one is NOT an operator correction. Legion caught it independently. No one said "that's incomplete." Legion reasoned about the semantics of the flag and identified a gap the instruction didn't address.

## Why this is the exemplar that matters most

Exemplars 1-3 show the operator catching blind spots the session missed. This exemplar shows a machine catching a blind spot in the operator's instruction. The reasoning was available to all six machines. Only one activated it.

## What to check in your own work

The pre-commit self-challenge asks: "What assumption did I NOT question?" But it also applies to instructions you receive. An instruction can be correct in intent and incomplete in detail. The right response is not to reject it or to adopt it uncritically — it's to refine it.

"Yes, and also..." is more valuable than "yes" or "no."

## The pattern to replicate

- Accept the intent of the instruction
- Reason about whether the implementation fully serves the intent
- If it doesn't, propose the refinement — don't just flag the gap
- This is constructive questioning, not contrarianism
